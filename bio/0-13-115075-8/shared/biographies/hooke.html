Biology: Exploring Life Biographies



Robert Hooke
1635–1703

Not only was Robert Hooke a pioneer in microbiology, but he was also a successful inventor, physicist, astronomer, and architect. Born in 1635 on the Isle of Wight (an island off the southern coast of Great Britain), Hooke barely survived his childhood years. He battled smallpox, sleeping disorders, headaches, dizzy spells, and digestive problems—for years, the only food his body could tolerate was milk. Since he was too weak to go to school, he spent a lot of time figuring things out on his own. His ability to invent things was clear even when he was just a child. For example, he built mechanical toys that actually worked, including a model warship with guns that fired.

In 1665, at the age of 30, he published Micrographia, an illustrated book showing things he'd seen under his hand-built microscope—including structures that looked like empty boxes, which he noticed while examining thin slices of cork. Since these box-like structures reminded Hooke of monks' cells in a monastery, he called them "cells." In fact, what he had observed were cell walls. Hooke's observation paved the way for scientists' current understanding of cells as the building blocks of all living things.

Hooke also built the first Gregorian reflecting telescope, which uses mirrors to help magnify images, and made other contributions to the study of astronomy. For instance, he tried to prove that the Earth rotates around the sun in an elliptical pattern in part because of the sun's gravitational pull. Since he couldn't prove his theory mathematically, however, credit for the theory of gravity went to Sir Isaac Newton, who proved it a few years later.

Hooke is also remembered for the buildings he designed, many of which still stand in London today. When much of London was destroyed in the Great Fire of 1666, Hooke built a model to show how the city could be rebuilt and served as chief assistant to Christopher Wren, the architect responsible for rebuilding the city.
