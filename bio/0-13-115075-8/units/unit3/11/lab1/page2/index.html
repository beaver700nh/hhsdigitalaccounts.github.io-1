Introduction
You will start your investigation of strawberry DNA by taking a look at what happens to molecules of DNA in a test tube during the last steps of the extraction procedure you will perform in the lab. At this point in the procedure, your test tube will contain distinct layers of two different liquids. The red layer at the bottom of the test tube is an extract from mashed strawberries. The clear layer above it is ethanol, a solvent that repels DNA molecules.

Background
Every cell in a strawberry contains eight copies of each of its chromosomes. As a result, strawberries contain large amounts of DNA. To extract the DNA, strawberry cells are first broken apart mechanically, by crushing. Next, detergents are added to dissolve the cell membranes. A filtering step then removes cell organelles, broken cell walls, membrane fragments, and other cell debris. After the filtering step, what remains is a red-colored solution containing DNA and other small dissolved molecules such as sugars and proteins. When cold ethanol is layered on top of this solution, molecules of ethanol repel the DNA molecules and the DNA clumps together. A ropelike clump of DNA molecules forms that is large enough to see with the unaided eye. In the lab, you will lift the DNA out of the test tube with a glass rod or inoculating loop.
