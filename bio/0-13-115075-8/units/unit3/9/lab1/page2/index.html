Background
Before the modern compound light microscope, biologists had very limited capabilities to see the inner-workings of a cell. Early simple microscopes (containing only one lens) could only magnify an image up to about 266X. However, with the development of the modern compound light microscope (containing an eyepiece lens and an objective lens) in the late 19th century, researchers could see images magnified by 1000X. Researchers also took advantage of new dyes that became available in the 1800s to stain cells for observation under the microscope. These stains made it possible to see structures inside cells, such as the nucleus. The word nucleus means "a central point or mass." The nucleus got its name because it was the prominent stained object seen in the middle of each cell. The stains also enabled biologists to observe structures, now known to be chromosomes, that underwent changes during cell division.

19th-Century Cell Discoveries
In 1888, your "colleague," German biologist Heinrich Waldeyer, coined the term chromosome (Greek chroma=color, soma=body) to describe the colored bodies he observed in stained dividing cells. You decide to find out how other 19th-century scientists have described these "colored bodies" or chromosomes. You look in scientific papers published by biologists from Germany, Russia and France, and note the discoveries below.

Wilhelm Hofmeister (Germany)
Observed lumps or nuggets, which appeared inside the cell and eventually separated into two masses. In German, he called these structures Klumpen.

Walther Flemming (Germany)
Observed cell structures he called Knauel that looked like skeins or tufts of yarn. He also saw these skeins or tufts form a star shape.

Edmund Russow (Russia)
Observed structures in dividing cells he described in German as Stabchen, meaning "small rods." The rods were bright and highly refractive (they distorted light) when stained.

Edouard-Gérard Balbiani (France)
Observed cell structures he called batonets etroits that looked like narrow little batons of different sizes.
