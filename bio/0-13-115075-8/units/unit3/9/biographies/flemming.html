Walther Flemming
(You can read more about Walther Flemming in the Exploring Life Chapter 9 Feature: The Discovery of Chromosomes.)

After Theodor Schwann determined that every cell contains a nucleus, scientists began their quest to view the inside of the nucleus. Carl Nageli observed chromosomes in 1842, but he disregarded them, thinking they were merely cell clutter created when he prepared the cell.

In 1856, a purple chemical called aniline dye, a bi–product of coal tar, was discovered. The dye was an instant hit in the production of clothing. A German scientist, Walther Flemming, thought the dye might help make the parts of cells more visible. Flemming, born in 1843, served as a physician in the Franco–Prussian War and later took jobs as a physiologist at the University of Prague and the University of Kiel in Germany. He found that the aniline dye stained the nuclear material a darker color than the rest of the cell. He was now able to differentiate the various nuclear structures. In 1879, he called the convoluted, string–like material he observed chromatin.

Working with the embryo cells of salamanders, the stain allowed Flemming to view the large nuclei clearly. He was able to watch these cells divide, and he even observed that the nuclear material condensed into threadlike objects. He called these structures "chromatin threads." Today we call them chromosomes. Flemming learned that embryo cells divide in a fixed time frame, and he was therefore able to stain the cells in the various stages of division. He called this process mitosis. The terms that Flemming used for the stages of mitosis and his descriptions of the process are still used today.
