Introduction
To start your investigation, you will take a closer look at the structure of yeast cells found in bread dough. You will find out how the yeast cells in bread dough use the fermentation process to get energy for life processes and see how they make bread dough rise. In the lab, you will measure the rate of fermentation by yeast in samples of bread dough containing different concentrations of sugar. You will place the dough samples into five small canisters and mark the initial levels of the dough. You will place the canisters in an "incubator" to warm the dough. Then you will mark the levels to which the dough rises every 5 minutes.

Background
Yeasts are single-celled fungi that live in liquid or moist habitats. Some yeasts live on the surface of plants (especially fruit), others live on animal tissue, and others live in the soil. In this lab, you will work with baker's yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae, a kind of yeast used to raise bread dough.

Yeast cells require sugar as a source of energy. In the presence of oxygen, yeast cells perform cellular respiration. However, when they are surrounded by bread dough, yeast cells do not have access to oxygen. The yeast cells perform fermentation instead of cellular respiration, producing ethyl alcohol, carbon dioxide gas, and the energy-storing molecule ATP. As the yeast cells release carbon dioxide, the gas is trapped in the dough. Bubbles form in the dough and cause the dough to rise.
