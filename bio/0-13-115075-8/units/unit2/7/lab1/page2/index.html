Introduction
Have you ever roasted marshmallows and accidentally set one on fire? You may have been amazed by the size of the flame that the marshmallow fueled! All food contains stored energy that can be released when the food is burned. To investigate the chemical energy stored in food, you need a calorimeter—an apparatus that measures the calorie content of food samples. Recall that a calorie is defined as the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1g of water by 1°C. (Note that the "calorie" counts listed on food packaging labels are given in kilocalories [kcal]. One kilocalorie is equal to 1000 calories.) It is also useful to know that different types of molecules can store different amounts of energy. While proteins and carbohydrates contain 4 kcal/g, fats contain 9 kcal/g.
