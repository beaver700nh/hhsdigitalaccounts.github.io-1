OBJECTIVE: to understand how oxygen and hydrogen react to release energy

You might be surprised to learn that during cellular respiration, the hydrogen atoms in food, such as sugar, react with oxygen and release energy, as in the Hindenburg explosion. Then why don't you explode in a ball of flames? The reason is that the energy in sugar is released in a chain of small, controlled steps, in a location where the energy can be harnessed to perform useful cellular work, instead of in a one-step explosion.

The following animation contrasts the combustion of sugar with the small steps by which cells release the energy of sugar. In cells, electrons pass from sugar to NADH and then to electron transport chains. Orange lines represent the change in potential energy as electrons travel from hydrogen in sugar to oxygen.
