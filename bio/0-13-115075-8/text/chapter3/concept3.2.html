Have you ever heard the phrase "nature versus nurture"? This expression refers to the idea that behavior is due either to genes (nature) or to environmental influences (nurture). For example, is the digger wasp born "knowing" how to use landmarks to find its nests? Or, does each wasp learn this skill during its lifetime? The answer is more complex than either/or. All of an animal's behaviors depend on some combination of genes and environment.

Innate Behaviors
Researchers have observed that digger wasps raised in isolation build nests in the same way as wasps raised among other wasps. The conclusion is that a digger wasp is born "knowing" how to build a nest—it does not have to learn this behavior from another wasp. A behavior that is performed correctly by all individuals of a species, even if they have no previous experience with the behavior, is called an innate behavior.

Note that this concept of innate behavior does not mean that such behaviors are only determined by genes. Even the most programmed behavior is influenced by the environment. For example, perhaps the appropriate type of sandy nesting site must be available to trigger the wasp's behavior. Furthermore, the wasp has to have the physical skills to carry out the behavior. Both genetic and environmental factors (such as nutrition) contributed to the development of the insect's nervous and muscular systems that together enable it to perform the behavior.

Niko Tinbergen and Austrian biologist Konrad Lorenz observed innate behaviors in many organisms. One famous study involved an interesting behavior in the graylag goose. If the goose bumps one of her eggs out of her nest area, she always uses her beak to retrieve it in the same way (Figure 3-6). When Lorenz and Tinbergen pulled the egg away from the goose during this process, she continued as though the egg were still there. Only after the goose sat down on her nest did she seem to notice that the egg was still outside the nest, and she started the process all over again. The scientists even substituted other objects, such as small toys or balls, for the egg, yet the goose did not alter her behavior.

Figure 3-6
The graylag goose responds to an egg outside her nest with a set of specific actions. Even when the egg is removed, the goose will continue to push at the air with her beak until she completes the FAP (fixed action pattern).

The goose's egg-retrieving response is an example of a fixed action pattern (abbreviated FAP)—an innate behavior that occurs as an unchangeable sequence of actions. An animal can only perform a FAP as a whole "script," from beginning to end. Once an animal starts a particular FAP, it usually completes the sequence no matter what happens along the way.

A FAP is usually triggered by a specific stimulus. In the goose example, the stimulus is the sight of an egg (or other similar object) outside the nest. What is the ultimate cause of such behavior? Why might a FAP evolve, even if it could cause the goose to roll a strange object into her nest? A FAP allows an animal to perform a task correctly the first time, without learning the behavior. You might hypothesize that it would increase an organism’s likelihood of success if it could perform important tasks without practicing first. Observations that FAPs often involve activities that are critical to survival and reproductive success, such as feeding or defending offspring, support this hypothesis.

Rhythms of Behavior
Another example of the interplay of programmed behavior with environmental influences is the ability of many animals to synchronize their activities with their environment. For example, if you have traveled to another time zone, you may have experienced "jet lag"—a period when you were "out of sync" with your surroundings. However, within a few days, you were adjusted to the night and day cues in the environment of the new time zone.

Animals exhibit a great variety of rhythmic (regularly repeated) behavior patterns that are synchronized to rhythmic changes in environmental factors. The patterns may be seasonal, such as mammals that migrate to better feeding grounds during dry seasons. Or they may be daily, such as flying squirrels that are active at night and sleep from dawn to dusk.

To study the natural rhythms of flying squirrels, researchers placed several squirrels in cages containing running wheels. The researchers traced the activity patterns of the squirrels in two different light conditions for 23 days (Figure 3-8). The control-group graph shows the activity pattern of a squirrel exposed to 12 hours of light followed by 12 hours of darkness. The experimental-group graph shows the activity pattern of a squirrel kept in darkness.

Figure 3-8
A quantitative experiment with flying squirrels supports the hypothesis that environmental cues influence the animal's activity rhythms. The wheel in each squirrel's cage was connected to a machine that recorded wheel turns, allowing researchers to track active periods of the squirrels. These graphs display the data collected each 24-hour period from noon to noon. Control-group squirrels were kept in 12 hours of light and 12 hours of darkness, while the experimental-group squirrels were kept in constant darkness.

Both squirrels had a distinct active period every day. The control squirrel became active at about the same time each day. In contrast, the experimental squirrel's high activity period shifted about 21 minutes later each day. After 23 days, the period had shifted nearly 8 hours from that of the control. When this squirrel returned to a regular cycle of 12 hours of light and 12 hours of dark, its activity cycle matched that of the control squirrel within a few days. These results suggest that without environmental cues, the squirrels kept a daily rhythm of 24 hours, 21 minutes. This type of innate rhythm with a cycle of about 24 hours is called a circadian rhythm (sur KAY dee uhn). Circadian rhythms are controlled by an organism's internal "biological clock." But note the environmental influence: Daily cycles of light and dark set the organism's biological clock to a cycle of exactly 24 hours. This interaction of environment with genetic programs keeps the squirrel's body rhythms coordinated with its external world.

Investigating the Interaction of Genes and Experience: A Case Study
How can researchers design an experiment to analyze the roles of inheritance and experience in forming a particular behavior? One way is to choose a subject in which you can control the genetic contribution by mating certain organisms. For example, the two closely-related species of lovebirds in Figure 3-9 both build their nests with strips of vegetation cut with their beaks. The Fischer's lovebird cuts long strips and carries them back to the nest site one at a time in its beak. In contrast, the peach-faced lovebird cuts short strips and carries several at a time by tucking them into its feathers. Are these techniques examples of innate behavior, or do the birds learn them from experience?

Figure 3-9
An experiment with lovebirds demonstrated that hybrid offspring could alter inherited behavior based on experience.

To find the answer, biologists designed an experiment that mated the two lovebird species to produce offspring (called hybrids). A hybrid lovebird cuts strips that are in between the lengths of strips cut by the two parent species. And the hybrid even attempts to carry the strips using a combined technique. For example, the hybrid usually tries tucking the strips under its feathers without releasing them from its beak. After failing to transport the strips under its feathers, it will learn to carry a strip in its beak. But the hybrid will still act out part of the tucking sequence by turning its head to the rear before flying off.

This experiment suggests that the differences in the behavior of the two species are inherited. However, the observation that the hybrid birds could modify their behavior and build a nest suggests that learning also plays a role in shaping the behavior.
