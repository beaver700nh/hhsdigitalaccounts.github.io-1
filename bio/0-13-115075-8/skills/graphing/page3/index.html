Graphing

OBJECTIVE: to read, interpret, and make a circle graph

Circle graphs, like bar graphs, compare data from different categories. Sometimes called pie charts, circle graphs display data as parts of a whole. However, to use a circle graph, you must have data that add up to 100%. For example, you could use a circle graph to represent the favorite sports of the students in one class, with each sector of the circle representing a different sport. In this activity, you will interpret a circle graph and then build one of your own. When you have completed the activity, scroll down and answer the questions below.

Answer the following questions in the boxes provided. After you complete each answer, click the Check Your Answer button for feedback.

1. How do you know which number to read on the protractor when you are measuring an angle?

2. When would it be more appropriate to use a circle graph than a bar graph?

3. If you were showing the relative proportions of landmass made up by Earth's seven continents would you use a bar graph or a circle graph? Explain.

4. If you were comparing the numbers of licensed drivers in six different states, would you use a bar graph or a circle graph? Explain.

5. Why must all the data for a circle graph add up to 100%?
